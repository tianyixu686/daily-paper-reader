# When Do Multimodal and Graph-Augmented RAG Help? A Controlled Evaluation for Document Question Answering 

Sokipriala Jonah 

Centre for Computational Science and Mathematical Modelling Coventry University 

Coventry, United Kingdom 

jonahs@uni.coventry.ac.uk 

Abstract —Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems commonly operate on text extracted from documents, poten-tially losing information contained in figures, tables, layout, and relationships distributed across passages. We present an explainable multimodal graph-RAG architecture that augments a text-only baseline with LLM-extracted subject–relation–object triples and CLIP-based retrieval of figures and tables. The three evidence sources are retrieved independently and fused only at generation time, allowing the effects of graph evidence, visual evidence, and generator choice to be evaluated separately. We conduct a controlled four-way ablation on 1,000 PubLayNet pages across single-passage, multi-hop, and figure questions using two closed-weight and two open-weight multimodal generators. We also compare matched caption-answerable and pixel-only figure question sets to distinguish caption recovery from gen-uine visual question answering. Knowledge-graph augmentation provides no reliable accuracy improvement across generators or question types in this corpus. On pixel-only questions, text-only systems achieve zero accuracy, while multimodal systems reach 0.057–0.114 and are constrained by both image retrieval (Recall@3 = 0.371 ) and the generators’ ability to interpret dense scientific figures and tables. Caption-derived questions substantially overestimate text-only visual question-answering capability. Processing the same image also produces an eleven-fold variation in input tokens across generators, showing that image tokenisation can dominate deployment cost. Source code, experiment configurations, and evaluation resources are publicly available at https://github.com/sokistar24/multimodal-graph-rag. These findings show that the value of multimodal and graph augmentation depends on retrieval design, corpus structure, benchmark construction, and generator efficiency. 

Index Terms —retrieval-augmented generation, knowledge graphs, multimodal retrieval, vision–language models, explain-ability, document question answering 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds large lan-guage models (LLMs) in external knowledge by retrieving evidence at query time [1], [2]. Conventional RAG systems typically operate on extracted text that is divided into chunks, embedded, and indexed for similarity search. Although ef-fective for many factual questions, this representation can lose information contained in figures, tables, spatial layout, and relationships distributed across passages. Optical character recognition can further distort document structure and numer-ical content. Consequently, a system may retrieve relevant text while still lacking the evidence required to answer correctly. This limitation is consistent with findings that retrieval alone leaves substantial factual errors unresolved and that preserving document pixels can improve downstream question answering [3], [4]. Multimodal RAG addresses this limitation by retrieving visual evidence alongside text and allowing a vision-language model to interpret document images, figures, and tables [5], [6]. Graph-based RAG instead represents entities and relations explicitly to support reasoning across distributed evidence [7]. Recent systems extend multimodal retrieval to multi-page and multi-document question answering [8], while large-scale benchmarks compare multimodal retrievers and open-weight and closed-weight generators [9]. Other studies increasingly combine multimodal evidence with graph structure or evaluate retrieval over multimodal knowledge graphs [10], [11]. These developments demonstrate the potential of multi-modal and graph-based RAG, but they also make system-level comparisons difficult to interpret. Performance can change because of the retriever, the evidence supplied to the model, the generator, or the construction of the evaluation questions. For example, a visual retriever may return a topically related figure that does not contain the requested value, while graph retrieval may introduce unrelated facts or restructure evidence already available in the retrieved text. Figure-question benchmarks may also measure caption recovery rather than direct inter-pretation of visual content. A controlled design is therefore needed to separate these effects. This study evaluates multimodal and graph augmentation within a common late-fusion RAG pipeline. The corpus contains 1,000 scientific document pages derived from Pub-LayNet [12]. Each page is represented through three evidence sources: OCR-derived text passages, LLM-extracted subject– relation–object triples, and cropped figures and tables embed-ded with CLIP. The sources are retrieved independently and combined only at generation time. The text index, visual index, knowledge graph, and retrieval parameters remain fixed across experimental conditions. We compare four configurations: a text-only baseline, text arXiv:2607.16604v1 [eess.IV] 18 Jul 2026 with graph evidence, text with visual evidence, and a combined multimodal graph-RAG system. The comparison is conducted across four vision-capable generators, comprising two closed-weight and two open-weight models, and across single-passage, multi-hop, and figure questions. Figure questions are additionally constructed under caption-answerable and pixel-only protocols to distinguish textual answer recovery from direct visual interpretation. The principal contributions of this paper are as follows:  

> •

A controlled evaluation of multimodal and graph augmentation. We introduce a late-fusion framework in which text, graph, and visual evidence can be included independently while the retrieval stack remains fixed. This supports a four-way ablation that separates the effects of evidence configuration from generator choice and records the evidence used for each answer.  

> •

A modality-aware protocol for evaluating figure ques-tions. We construct matched caption-answerable and pixel-only question sets using the same corpus, visual crops, and retriever. This design distinguishes caption-based answer recovery from question answering that requires direct interpretation of figures and tables.  

> •

A joint effectiveness and efficiency evaluation across generators. We compare open-weight and closed-weight multimodal generators using retrieval quality, answer accuracy, faithfulness, relevancy, latency, token usage, and cost. This provides a controlled assessment of how evidence configuration, generator capability, and image processing affect multimodal document question answer-ing. II. R ELATED WORK 

A. Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Evaluation 

RAG combines retrieval with generation so that answers are grounded in external evidence rather than parametric memory alone [1], [13]. However, retrieval does not guarantee correct generation. The CRAG benchmark reports substantial performance degradation on dynamic, less popular, and multi-hop questions [3]. This motivates evaluating retrieval and generation separately and stratifying results by question type. We assess answer accuracy, faithfulness, and relevancy using LLM judges following the RAGAS framework [14]. Judge models are kept separate from the generators under comparison to reduce self-evaluation bias. 

B. Multimodal Document RAG 

Multimodal RAG extends text retrieval with visual evidence from figures, tables, and page layouts. MMed-RAG combines domain-aware retrieval with adaptive context selection for medical vision-language models [5], while PixelRAG performs retrieval and reading directly from document screenshots [4]. ColPali similarly represents document pages as visual embed-dings, avoiding dependence on text extraction alone [6]. Recent document benchmarks provide broader evaluations of multimodal retrieval and generation. M3DocRAG sup-ports single-page, multi-page, and multi-document question answering using multimodal page retrieval [8]. MMDocRAG evaluates multimodal evidence selection and generation across a large collection of open-weight and closed-weight models and retrieval systems [9]. These studies demonstrate the impor-tance of preserving visual information, but primarily compare retrieval methods, generators, or end-to-end systems. Our approach retains text as the primary retrieval channel and uses CLIP [15] to retrieve figures and tables. Visual evi-dence is provided either as image pixels or captions, allowing direct visual reasoning to be distinguished from caption-based answer recovery. 

C. Graph-Enhanced and Multimodal Graph RAG 

GraphRAG introduced LLM-extracted entity graphs for query-focused summarisation and reasoning across distributed evidence [7]. Related approaches increasingly combine graph structure with multimodal information. MKG-RAG-Bench evaluates retrieval and downstream generation over multi-modal knowledge graphs in general and medical domains [10]. Wang and Chen construct multimodal document graphs to support long-range reasoning over visually rich documents [11]. These systems construct or retrieve from multimodal graphs in which visual and textual information are represented within a shared structure. In contrast, our architecture maintains separate text, graph, and visual retrieval branches and com-bines their outputs only at generation time. The knowledge graph contains cached subject–relation–object triples stored in NetworkX [16]. This late-fusion design enables the graph and visual branches to be removed independently while preserving identical text retrieval. 

D. Generator Comparisons in Multimodal RAG 

The generator can substantially affect how retrieved evi-dence is interpreted. Closed-weight models such as GPT-4o and Gemini provide multimodal capabilities through managed APIs [17], [18]. Open-weight models such as Llama 4 Scout and Llama 4 Maverick support text and image input and can be deployed through independent or hosted inference infrastructure [19]. MMDocRAG provides a large-scale comparison of open-weight and closed-weight models for multimodal document question answering [9]. Our study addresses a complementary question. Rather than maximising the number of evaluated models, we hold the corpus, retrieval indices, retrieved evi-dence, and prompt construction fixed while varying both the generator and the evidence configuration. This separates the effects of knowledge-graph evidence, visual evidence, and generator choice. The open-weight models in this study are accessed through hosted DeepInfra endpoints [20], [21]. The comparison there-fore concerns model access, serving behaviour, performance, and cost rather than local deployment. III. M ETHOD

B. Corpus and Preprocessing 

The corpus contains 1,000 pages from the 

small-publaynet-wds distribution hosted on Hugging Face [22]. This distribution is derived from PubLayNet, a large-scale dataset of scientific document pages with region-level layout annotations [12]. 

a) Text regions.: Regions labelled as text, title, list, or table are cropped and processed with Tesseract OCR. The resulting strings are combined into one text file per page, with the page identifier retained for provenance and retrieval evaluation. 

b) Visual regions.: Regions labelled as figures or tables are saved as image crops. Tables are therefore represented both as OCR-derived text and as images. This allows a text-only system to access tabular content when OCR is sufficiently accurate. 

c) Captions.: Each crop is captioned once during inges-tion using GPT-4o with vision. The model is prompted to produce one concise sentence describing the visible content, including chart type, axes, or table values where available. These captions are used by the caption-mediated generation path and by the caption-derived figure question protocol de-scribed in Section IV-B. OCR output is divided into overlapping windows of 500 characters with a 50-character overlap. Fixed-length windows are used because the extracted text contains inconsistent punctuation and structural boundaries. Text chunks are em-bedded using text-embedding-3-small [23], while visual crops are embedded using CLIP ViT-B/32 [15]. The embeddings are stored in separate FAISS indices and retrieved using cosine similarity [24]. Both indices are constructed once and reused across all experiments. 

C. Retrieval and Evidence Fusion 

Figure 1 summarises the full pipeline. All evidence sources are retrieved separately and then concatenated into one gener-ation prompt. 

1) Text baseline: The baseline retrieves the top k = 3 text chunks and generates an answer using only those passages. The prompt instructs the model to abstain when the supplied context does not contain the answer. 

2) Knowledge graph: GPT-4o-mini [25] extracts subject– relation–object triples from each text chunk. The triples are stored in a directed NetworkX graph [16]. At query time, entities occurring as whole words in the question are matched, and up to eight neighbouring triples are added to the generation prompt. Two filters reduce incidental matches. First, entities shorter than five characters are excluded. Second, a graph fact is included only when its source page appears among the pages returned by text retrieval. This provenance filter prevents un-related facts from other documents from entering the prompt. 

3) Multimodal retrieval: CLIP ranks figure and table crops by text-image similarity. Retrieval quality is measured at depth 

m = 5 , while the generator receives either the highest-ranked crop as pixels or the captions of the top three crops as text. Keeping scoring depth separate from prompt payload preserves rank information without sending multiple images to the generator. The two visual-input variants use the same CLIP ranking:  

> •

Pixel input: the highest-ranked crop is supplied directly to the vision-language model.  

> •

Caption-mediated input: the captions of the top three crops are supplied as text, with no image attached. Pixel input is used only for figure questions. For text and multi-hop questions, the multimodal configurations use captions. This gating prevents an arbitrary image from being attached to queries that do not require visual evidence. 

D. Explainability 

For each answer, the system records the retrieved text chunks and source pages, the graph facts added to the prompt, the retrieved visual region and its CLIP score, and a brief account of how the evidence was combined. The trace can be reported as concise provenance or expanded to include chunk excerpts. Although explainability is not evaluated as a separate quantitative outcome, these logs support the error analysis in Section IV-G. IV. E XPERIMENTS AND RESULTS

The evaluation follows a 4 × 4 × 3 design comprising four system configurations, four production generators, and three question types. The retrieval indices and rankings are fixed across systems and generators. This design isolates the effects of graph evidence, visual evidence, and generator choice. B. Question Sets 

The main ablation contains 100 questions drawn from the 1,000-page corpus: 35 single-passage text questions, 30 multi-hop questions requiring two facts from different parts of a page, and 35 pixel-only figure questions. A separate set of 35 caption-answerable figure questions is used to examine the effect of question construction. Question generation was separated from the models under evaluation. DeepSeek-Chat generated the text and multi-hop questions, while Claude Haiku 4.5 generated the figure ques-tions. All questions were produced with a fixed seed and temperature 0. Each question was required to identify the relevant variable, cohort, or measurement so that it remained unambiguous within a corpus of unrelated pages. References to a figure, table, chart, or other modality cue were prohibited because such wording would reveal which retrieval branch should be used. These constraints were enforced programmati-cally. Figure questions were verified against their source crops by a separate model call, and all questions were manually reviewed. 

1) Figure Question Protocols: Two figure question sets were constructed from the same corpus, crop pool, and visual retriever. They differ only in the information shown to the question author. 

a) Caption-answerable questions.: The author received only the GPT-4o caption associated with the crop. Questions were therefore limited to information expressed in the caption, typically a description of the figure’s subject or content. 

b) Pixel-only questions.: The author received the crop itself and was instructed to ask for information that required direct visual inspection, such as a table entry, axis value, or reported percentage. Each set contains 35 questions drawn from the same pool of 598 crops. The matched design allows the effect of question construction to be examined without changing the corpus, retriever, or generators.

E. Overall Ablation Results 

Table II reports the four-way ablation across all production generators. Text retrieval is identical across system configura-tions, with Recall@3 of 0.914 and MRR of 0.886 for text questions, and Recall@3 of 0.900 and MRR of 0.883 for multi-hop questions. Differences within each generator block therefore arise from the evidence added to the prompt rather than from changes in text retrieval. For text questions, accuracy ranges from 0.514 to 0.714, with no configuration consistently outperforming the baseline. Multi-hop accuracy ranges from 0.367 to 0.633 and also shows no stable ordering across systems or generators. On pixel-only figure questions, both text-only configurations score zero accuracy for every generator. Adding visual retrieval raises accuracy to 0.057–0.114, which confirms that image access is necessary for this question type but does not by itself produce reliable visual question answering.

This result reflects the structure of the data and the graph lookup procedure. Without provenance filtering, common en-tity strings can introduce facts from unrelated articles. With provenance filtering, graph facts are restricted to pages already returned by text retrieval and often restate information present in the retrieved passages. PubLayNet contains pages from unrelated articles and does not provide the shared cross-document entity space in which graph traversal is most likely to be useful. I. Efficiency 

Table V reports token use and cost on the pixel-only figure set. The same question, text context, and image are supplied to every generator, yet the image produces markedly different token counts. For the multimodal configuration, input length ranges from 861 tokens for the Llama 4 models to 9,477 tokens for GPT-4o-mini, an elevenfold difference caused by provider-specific image tokenisation. This variation has a larger impact on cost than the quoted token price alone. Llama 4 Scout costs $0.0084 per 100 pixel-only questions and achieves accuracy of 0.114, while GPT-4o-mini costs $0.1429 and achieves accuracy of 0.086. Scout is therefore approximately seventeen times cheaper in this setting while also producing a higher score. Latency follows a different pattern. Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite is the fastest model, with observed latency of 440–876 ms across configurations. Llama 4 Maverick is the slowest at 1,876–4,087 ms, despite receiving the same token count as Llama 4 Scout. These results show that multimodal deploy-ment cost and latency cannot be inferred from the listed token price alone. Image tokenisation and serving configuration must also be measured. V. D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 

This study evaluated four RAG configurations across four multimodal generators using a fixed retrieval stack. The de-sign isolates the effects of graph and visual evidence from differences in generator capability and reveals three main implications for document question answering. First, benchmark construction strongly affects the apparent value of multimodal RAG. Text-only systems answered some caption-derived figure questions because the required infor-mation was already available in textual form. Their accuracy fell to zero when the answers could only be obtained from the image. Figure question benchmarks should therefore state whether questions were generated from captions or pixels and report how many reference answers are recoverable from captions. Without this distinction, improvements attributed to visual reasoning may partly reflect caption retrieval. Second, knowledge-graph augmentation provided no con-sistent improvement in this corpus. Unrestricted entity match-ing can introduce facts from unrelated documents, while provenance-restricted matching mainly reformulates informa-tion already contained in the retrieved passages. This does not imply that graph augmentation is generally ineffective. Rather, its usefulness depends on a corpus containing meaningful relations across documents. PubLayNet consists largely of unrelated article pages and therefore offers limited opportunity for graph traversal to recover additional evidence. Third, multimodal retrieval was necessary for questions whose answers existed only in pixels, but it was not sufficient for reliable question answering. CLIP often retrieved visually or semantically similar figures rather than the exact table or figure containing the requested value. Generators also struggled to extract precise numerical information from dense scientific visuals. The limited benefit of the higher-priced diagnostic model suggests that improving retrieval and doc-ument representation may be more important than increasing generator scale. The efficiency results further show that multimodal de-ployment cost cannot be inferred from token prices alone. The same image produced substantially different token counts across providers, while a lower-cost model achieved compet-itive accuracy. Image encoding, latency, and input expansion should therefore be measured directly when selecting a mul-timodal backend. Visual retrieval should also be applied selectively. Similarity search always returns an image, even when a question does not require one. Supplying that image can introduce irrelevant evidence and unnecessary cost. Gating visual input according to the question or retrieval confidence is therefore part of a sound multimodal RAG design. 

A. Limitations and Outlook 

The evaluation contains 30–35 questions per condition, so small differences may correspond to only a few examples. The experiments also use a single layout-analysis corpus dominated by biomedical articles, which limits generalisation to other document types and more strongly connected collec-tions. Answer quality was assessed using binary LLM judges rather than human annotation, and the evaluated open-weight models were accessed through hosted APIs rather than local deployment. Future work should evaluate larger, human-validated ques-tion sets across multiple document domains. For tables, a promising direction is to match queries against OCR-derived cell content and return the associated image crop, combining textual precision with visual context. Graph augmentation should be tested on collections with genuine cross-document relations, such as citation networks or linked records.
