# D2PLAN : Dual-Agent Dynamic Global Planning for Complex Retrieval-Augmented Reasoning 

Kangcheng Luo, Tinglang Wu and Yansong Feng *

WICT, Peking University 

{luokangcheng, wutinglang}@stu.pku.edu.cn, fengyansong@pku.edu.cn 

Abstract 

Recent search-augmented LLMs trained with reinforcement learning (RL) can interleave searching and reasoning for multi-hop rea-soning tasks. However, they face two criti-cal failure modes as the accumulating context becomes flooded with both crucial evidence and irrelevant information: (1) ineffective search chain construction that produces incor-rect queries or omits retrieval of critical infor-mation, and (2) reasoning hijacking by periph-eral evidence that causes models to misidentify distractors as valid evidence. To address these challenges, we propose D 2 PLAN , a Dual-agent 

Dynamic global Plan ning paradigm for com-plex retrieval-augmented reasoning. D 2 PLAN 

operates through the collaboration of a Rea-soner and a Purifier : the Reasoner constructs explicit global plans during reasoning and dy-namically adapts them based on retrieval feed-back; the Purifier assesses retrieval relevance and condenses key information for the Rea-soner . We further introduce a two-stage train-ing framework consisting of supervised fine-tuning (SFT) cold-start on synthesized trajecto-ries and RL with plan-oriented rewards to teach LLMs to master the D 2 PLAN paradigm. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate that D 2 PLAN 

enables more coherent multi-step reasoning and stronger resilience to irrelevant informa-tion, thereby achieving superior performance on challenging QA benchmarks. 

1 Introduction 

Recent advancements in reinforcement learning (RL) have empowered Large Language Models (LLMs) with the capability to perform autonomous retrieval during reasoning (Song et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Transcend-ing the traditional Retrieval-Augmented Genera-tion (RAG) paradigm (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao

et al., 2023), these models are no longer passive consumers of retrieved documents. Instead, they actively determine when , what , and how to search, subsequently integrating the retrieved information into their reasoning processes (Lin et al., 2025), thereby enhancing performance on knowledge-intensive multi-hop reasoning tasks. However, we observe that as LLMs iteratively invoke retrievers to gather evidence for complex questions, the context becomes inundated with doc-uments retrieved at various steps. This lengthy context, containing both key evidence and substan-tial irrelevant information, dilutes the model’s at-tention to the cues in the original question and retrieved critical evidence (Hsieh et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025). As shown in Figure 1 (Left), the model loses track of temporal constraints in the original question, leading to a newly constructed query that lacks global context and causes a drift in retrieval targets. In other scenarios (Figure 1, Right), when faced with a lengthy context flooded with all retrieved information, the model struggles to discriminate between valid evidence and distrac-tors, leading to erroneous conclusions derived from irrelevant information. We categorize these perva-sive failure modes into two primary types: (E1) In-

4722 effective Search Chain Construction: The query sequences iteratively generated by models often contain incorrect queries, causing divergence from the original reasoning objective, or lack necessary queries, omitting retrieval of essential knowledge points. (E2) Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Evidence: Models tend to misidentify irrelevant information in retrieved results as valid evidence, and fail to re-retrieve when retrieval yields entirely irrelevant results. After analyzing the results of state-of-the-art methods, we find that both E1 and E2 are prevalent and account for 29% and 63% of total failures, re-spectively (§4.6). These analyzes suggest that to better deal with complex multi-hop reasoning tasks, models need precise perception and control over the retrieval-augmented reasoning process. Specifi-cally, two core capabilities are essential: (C1) Dy-namic Global Planning: The model must be ca-pable of formulating an explicit global plan and maintaining the awareness of this plan throughout the reasoning process. Furthermore, it should be able to dynamically adapt this plan based on re-trieval feedback, including revising sub-questions and rewriting queries when needed. This ability prevents drift in retrieval targets and the omission of critical reasoning or retrieval steps, while en-abling recovery from retrieval failures. (C2) Ro-bust Resistance to Peripheral Evidence: The model should be able accurately distinguish key ev-idence from irrelevant information in retrieved re-sults. This capability ensures reasoning is grounded in reliable evidence and enables the model to rec-ognize retrieval failures, thereby performing self-correction by triggering plan adaptation in C1. To equip models with such capabilities, we propose D 2PLAN , a Dual-agent Dynamic global 

Plan ning paradigm supported by a two-stage train-ing framework. D 2PLAN operates through the col-laboration of two agents: a Reasoner and a Puri-fier . The Reasoner performs retrieval-augmented reasoning with explicit dynamic global planning, while the Purifier evaluates retrieval relevance and distills key information for the Reasoner . For a given question, the Reasoner autonomously con-structs a global plan (i.e., an ordered sequence of sub-questions), sequentially addresses each sub-question, and dynamically adapts the plan based on feedback from the Purifier .The training process of D 2PLAN consists of two phases: SFT Cold-Start and SP LAN RL. In the first phase, we design a pipeline to manually imple-ment the workflow of D 2PLAN and synthesize data using a powerful teacher model to fine-tune both the Reasoner and Purifier . This cold-start stage enables the Reasoner to acquire preliminary rea-soning capabilities with dynamic global planning, and enables the Purifier to learn accurate relevance assessment and information condensation. In the second phase, we introduce SP LAN RL, which uti-lizes plan-oriented rewards to enhance the Rea-soner ’s reasoning capability by guiding it to cor-rectly formulate and adapt global plans. To validate the effectiveness of D 2PLAN , we conducted exten-sive experiments on challenging QA benchmarks. Notably, our method achieves an average absolute performance improvement of 3.8% over the state-of-the-art baseline across six datasets. Our contributions are as follows: (1) We iden-tify two prevalent failure modes of existing LLMs when handling lengthy retrieval-augmented reason-ing contexts: Ineffective Search Chain Construc-tion and Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Ev-idence. (2) We propose D 2PLAN , a dual-agent dynamic planning paradigm supported by a two-stage training framework, to enhance the global planning and resistance to peripheral evidence of search-augmented LLMs, thereby mitigating these failure modes. (3) Extensive experiments demon-strate that D 2PLAN enables more coherent query formulation and stronger resilience to peripheral evidence during reasoning, achieving superior per-formance on challenging QA benchmarks. 

2 Methodology 

In this section, we present our D 2PLAN paradigm and the corresponding training framework. First, we provide an overview of the D 2PLAN workflow 1,introducing how its dual-agent architecture and dy-namic global planning mechanism operate when solving challenging problems (§2.1). Then, we de-tail how we teach LLMs to master this paradigm through a two-stage training framework comprising SFT cold-start (§2.2) and SP LAN RL (§2.3).

Dynamic Global Planning As shown in Fig-ure 2, given an input question, the Reasoner au-tonomously assesses its complexity and constructs a global problem-solving plan. Single-hop ques-tions require no decomposition, whereas multi-hop questions are decomposed into an ordered sequence of sub-questions to form an initial plan. In this sequence, each sub-question depends only on pre-ceding ones and can reference their answers us-ing placeholders like #A_j (denoting the answer to the j-th sub-question), which allows the Reasoner 

to solve them sequentially. When solving each sub-question, the Reasoner can iteratively gener-ate queries and invoke search tools based on feed-back from the Purifier , continuing until relevant evidence is obtained or the maximum number of attempts K is reached. Upon successfully solving a sub-question, the Reasoner refines the subsequent sub-question based on the resolved context, mak-ing it more specific and self-contained to facilitate effective retrieval and prevent divergence from the original reasoning objective. Conversely, if a sub-question remains unsolved after reaching the max-imum retrieval attempts, the Reasoner revises the remaining sub-questions, including the current un-solved one, to explore alternative problem-solving paths for self-correction. 

2.2 SFT Cold-Start 

We use SFT to train the dual agents, guiding Rea-soner to acquire preliminary retrieval-augmented 4724 reasoning capabilities with dynamic global plan-ning, and enabling Purifier to master relevance assessment and information condensation. 

2.2.1 Synthesizing Trajectories 

To obtain high-quality SFT training data, we design a pipeline that guides a powerful teacher LLM to play the roles of Reasoner and Purifier .2 Specifi-cally, we employ a series of curated prompts (see Appendix N) to step-by-step guide the teacher model to simulate the workflow described in §2.1, executing a series of operations including question decomposition, query rewriting, plan refinement, and plan revision, etc. Through this process, we obtain a set of reasoning trajectories synthesized ac-cording to the D 2PLAN workflow. Each trajectory is also accompanied by the corresponding input-output pairs of the Purifier for relevance judgment and information purification at each retrieval step. 

2.2.2 Constructing SFT Data 

To construct high-quality SFT training data, we first filter the synthesized reasoning trajectories based on two criteria: (1) the final answer exactly matches the gold answer; (2) the trajectory contains at least one retrieval call. The second criterion ensures that the model prioritizes learning to leverage retrieval tools for problem-solving rather than relying on po-tentially erroneous internal parametric knowledge. Based on this filtered set of high-quality trajec-tories, we construct corresponding training data for both agents. For the Reasoner , we further use the teacher LLM to reformat the filtered reasoning trajectories, integrating the segmented reasoning steps synthesized via multiple prompts into a co-herent and fluent reasoning chain that serves as the training target. For the Purifier , we extract the input-output pairs corresponding to each retrieval step from the filtered reasoning trajectories as its training data. 

2.3 SP LAN RL 

After the SFT stage, we freeze the Purifier and introduce SP LAN RL to further enhance the Rea-soner ’s reasoning capability by strengthening its dynamic planning proficiency. In addition to the commonly used format and answer rewards in gen-eral RL, we design plan-oriented rewards to effec-tively guide the model’s dynamic planning behav-ior, incentivizing both the quality of plan construc-tion and the effectiveness of plan adjustments. 

> 2See Appendix B for detailed algorithms and pseudocode.

Initial Planning Reward This reward incen-tivizes the model to identify the required number of reasoning steps and construct high-quality initial plans. Specifically, for single-hop questions, we as-sign Rp = 1 if the model does not perform question decomposition, and Rp = 0 otherwise. For multi-hop questions, we assign Rp = 1 if the model performs question decomposition and the number of generated sub-questions matches the hop count annotated in the dataset, and Rp = 0 otherwise. We use count-based rather than semantic constraints for two reasons. First, count-based constraints do not require annotations of sub-questions and sub-answers, making them more practical to implement. Second, count-based constraints effectively prevent the model from generating redundant sub-questions or missing necessary ones, thereby improving the conciseness and effectiveness of planning (§4.4). 

Plan Adaptation Reward This reward aims to reinforce the model’s capability of dynamically adjusting plans throughout the reasoning process, encompassing two complementary mechanisms: 

plan refinement and plan revision .

Plan Refinement Reward. For each sub-question that depends on the preceding ones, we use the Plan Refinement Reward Rref ine to encourage the model to refine it after solving those dependencies— filling in placeholders and making subsequent sub-questions more specific and self-contained. A suc-cessful refinement requires that the refined sub-question exists in the reasoning trajectory and con-tains no placeholders, which we verify using regu-lar expression matching. If all such sub-questions are successfully refined, the reward Rref ine = 1 ,and Rref ine = 0 otherwise. This reward not only encourages the model to improve sub-question quality, but also effectively prevents the drift in retrieval targets during multi-hop reasoning. 

Plan Revision Reward. We design a revision re-ward to encourage the model to globally revise the remaining sub-questions when iterative retrieval reaches the maximum number of attempts with-out finding relevant information. Such failures are often caused by poorly formulated sub-questions, such as those that are overly vague or lack neces-sary constraints. This reward is jointly determined by timing reward Rt and quality reward Rq. Rt = 1 

if the model triggers revision only after retrieval reaches the maximum number of failures, prevent-ing premature abandonment; otherwise Rt = 0 .

Rq = 1 if the first revised sub-question (i.e., the current unsolved sub-question after revision) suc-4725 cessfully retrieves relevant information; otherwise 

Rq = 0 . We use the relevance judgment from the Purifier to determine whether the retrieval is successful. Specifically, let c ∈ { 0, 1} denote an-swer correctness and r ∈ { 0, 1} indicate whether revision was triggered, then:

For example, the model must wrap the initial plan with <plan></plan> tags, the revised plan with

where α and β are scaling factors that control the contribution of each reward component. 

3 Experimental Setup 

Datasets and Evaluation Metrics To compre-hensively evaluate the capabilities of D 2PLAN , we conduct experiments on widely used open-domain QA benchmarks, including Natural Questions (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), SimpleQA (Wei et al., 2024), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2Wiki-MultihopQA (2Wiki) (Ho et al., 2020), MuSiQue (MSQ) (Trivedi et al., 2022b), and FRAMES (Kr-ishna et al., 2025). For evaluation metrics, we employ Exact Match (EM) and LLM-as-a-Judge (LasJ). For LasJ, we utilize qwen-plus (Qwen et al., 2025) to assess the correctness of predicted answers (see Appendix N for the prompt). 

Baselines We compare D 2PLAN with represen-tative RL-based methods: Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), ReSearch (Chen et al., 2025), AutoRe-fine (Shi et al., 2025), ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025), and StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025). We reproduce the inference results of all baseline methods under the same environment to ensure fair comparison. 

Implementation Details We employ Qwen2.5-3B/7B-Instruct (Qwen et al., 2025) as the back-bone models. For retrieval, we utilize E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the embedding model and the 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020) as the retrieval corpus, retrieving the top-5 passages. For SFT cold-start, we use qwen-max-latest as the teacher model to synthesize 4,000 training in-stances for the Reasoner and 11,255 for the Puri-fier from the training sets of NQ, HotpotQA, and MuSiQue. For RL training, we sample 29,762 instances from the same training sets and adopt GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) as the learning algorithm, implemented on verl (Sheng et al., 2025). More im-plementation details are provided in Appendix C.

4.2 Ablation Study (RQ1) 

To investigate the contributions of each core com-ponent to model performance, we conduct ablation studies on D 2PLAN -3B and report the results in Table 2. Here, Plan refers to the initial global plan-ning mechanism for decomposing the question into sub-questions, and Adapt refers to the plan adapta-tion mechanism for plan refinement and revision. Detailed implementations of each ablation variant are provided in Appendix E. Results show that under the Only SFT setting, re-moving the planning components ( w/o Plan&Adapt 

or w/o Adapt ) significantly degrades performance compared to D 2PLAN . Despite being trained on reasoning trajectories synthesized from the same question set, the lack of dynamic planning mech-anism prevents these variants from effectively or-ganizing reasoning paths for complex multi-hop problems. This confirms that D 2PLAN ’s gains stem from learned dynamic planning capabilities rather than mere knowledge distillation from the teacher model. Comparing SFT+RL with Only SFT , RL consistently improves all variants; however, even with RL optimization, w/o Plan&Adapt still lags behind D 2PLAN , demonstrating that the dynamic planning paradigm provides irreplaceable advan-tages for structuring complex multi-hop reasoning. Furthermore, the performance drop caused by ex-cluding the initial planning reward Rp (w/o Rp)or the plan adaptation reward Ra (w/o Ra) under-scores the necessity of these rewards in guiding the model to internalize robust plan construction and adaptation capabilities.

4.6 Error Analysis (RQ5) 

To understand how D 2PLAN mitigates errors, we analyze error type distributions on FRAMES and MuSiQue, comparing it with Search-R1 and StepSearch. We divide (E2) Reasoning Hijack-ing by Peripheral Evidence into two subtypes: (E2.1) the model reasons with irrelevant retrieval results without attempting remediation; (E2.2) the model recognizes that the initial retrieval result is irrelevant and attempts query rewriting several times but still fails to obtain key evidence, result-ing in an incorrect answer. 3 Errors beyond E1 and E2 are categorized as (E3) Other Errors . We sample 200 questions each from FRAMES and MuSiQue, using gemini-3-pro-preview (Team et al., 2025) to identify the type of the first error in the reasoning trajectory. We manually annotated 100 samples, achieving a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.72, confirming Gemini’s reliability. As shown in Fig-ure 4, D 2PLAN achieves the lowest error rates by substantially reducing E1 and E2.1, validating the efficacy of dynamic global planning and robust re-sistance to peripheral evidence. The relative rise in E2.2 suggests that as planning and peripheral evidence handling improve, failures shift toward inherent retrieval limitations and the constrained reasoning capabilities of the 7B model. Manual verification of sampled E2.2 cases further confirms this: 14 out of 20 are caused by retrieval system limitations where gold documents are structurally absent from the corpus, rather than reasoning defi-ciencies of the model (see Appendix K for details). 

5 Related Work 

Planning in Retrieval-Augmented Generation. 

Planning is crucial for RAG systems to handle complex multi-hop questions. Iterative RAG meth-ods (Shao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) interleave reasoning and retrieval but typically perform local planning, leading to in-tent drift in lengthy histories. PlanRAG (Lee et al., 2024) introduces a structured approach that gener-ates plans and adjusts them via iterative re-planning. However, it relies on zero-shot prompting of mod-els without parameter optimization. 

Reinforcement Learning for Search-Augmented LLMs. Recently, RL has become the dominant paradigm for training search-augmented LLMs (Jin et al., 2025b; Song et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025), as it encourages autonomous exploration and better generalization than SFT (Chu et al., 2025). Further research intro-duces step-wise process supervision (Zheng et al.,

Despite these advances, current methods strug-gle with noisy and lengthy retrieval-augmented rea-soning contexts. Our method addresses these chal-lenges by introducing a dual-agent dynamic global planning paradigm, optimized via a two-stage train-ing framework. 

6 Conclusion 

We identify two prevalent failure modes of search-augmented LLMs in complex retrieval-augmented reasoning: Ineffective Search Chain Construction and Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Evidence. To address these issues, we introduce D 2PLAN ,a dual-agent dynamic planning paradigm where the Reasoner performs reasoning with explicit dy-namic planning and the Purifier evaluates retrieval relevance and distills key information. A two-stage training framework comprising SFT cold-start and planning-oriented RL equips LLMs with the D 2PLAN paradigm. Extensive experiments demonstrate that D 2PLAN enables more coher-ent query formulation and stronger resilience to peripheral evidence, achieving superior retrieval-augmented reasoning abilities. 

7 Limitations 

While D 2PLAN demonstrates strong performance on open-domain QA benchmarks, several limita-tions remain. First, although we achieve significant improvements on Qwen2.5-3B and Qwen2.5-7B models, due to computational cost constraints, we are unable to validate our method on larger lan-guage models. Second, due to cost limitations, we employ a local dense retriever with a static Wikipedia corpus rather than more powerful re-trieval systems such as commercial search engines (e.g., Google). As revealed in our error analysis, a notable portion of failures (E2.2) may stem from retriever or corpus limitations. Integrating stronger retrieval infrastructure could potentially further en-hance the performance of D 2PLAN . Third, our approach relies on a powerful teacher model to gen-erate high-quality reasoning trajectories for super-vised fine-tuning. This dependency may limit the applicability of our method in scenarios where ac-cess to such teacher models is restricted or costly.

F Format Reward Constraints 

The format reward Rf enforces the following con-straints to ensure parsable reasoning paths: 1. Answer Tag Constraint : The model output must contain exactly one pair of <answer> 

and </answer> tags, enclosing the final an-swer. 2. Plan Tag Constraint : For multi-hop ques-tions, the output must contain <plan> and 

</plan> tags that enclose the initial decompo-sition of sub-questions. 3. Sub-question Identifier Constraint : Within the plan, each sub-question must be marked with identifiers " #Q_i: " (where i is the sub-question index), enabling systematic tracking of the solving process. 4. Refinement Tag Constraint : When refin-ing a sub-question after solving its dependen-cies, the model must use <Updated_#Q_i> 

and </Updated_#Q_i> tags to mark the re-fined version. 5. Revision Tag Constraint : When triggering plan revision after retrieval failures, the model must use <Replan> and </Replan> tags to enclose the revised plan. These constraints ensure that the reasoning tra-jectory can be automatically parsed via regular ex-pressions to compute the Initial Planning Reward 

Rp (by extracting the initial plan and counting sub-questions) and the Plan Adaptation Reward Ra (by detecting refinement tags, revision tags, and itera-tive retrieval patterns).

H Purifier Transplantation to Baselines 

To disentangle the contribution of the Purifier from the overall D 2Plan framework, we added our trained Purifier (Qwen2.5-7B-SFT) to representa-tive baselines. Since baselines like Search-R1 and R1-Searcher lack sub-question decomposition, we use their search queries as surrogate target ques-tions for the Purifier’s relevance judgment (Algo-rithm 2), which is inherently less precise than a well-formed sub-question. As shown in Table 9, the Purifier brings modest improvements to baselines (e.g., +3.32% AVG for Search-R1), but they still fall far short of D 2Plan (+9.27% AVG over Search-R1+Purifier). This demonstrates that D 2Plan’s superior performance stems from the synergy between dynamic planning and the Purifier, rather than from either component alone. 

I Single-Agent Baseline Comparison 

To verify whether the explicit dual-agent workflow is essential, we trained a single-agent SFT base-line that performs retrieval, relevance assessment, compression, and reasoning all-in-one (merging the Purifier’s role into the Reasoner’s training data). Results on FRAMES: The single-agent with internal compression barely improves (+0.64%), while D 2Plan achieves +2.31% with a 63.6% reduction in context length. Internal compression actually increases context length (5,746 > 5,180) because raw documents must be loaded into the context before compression can occur, and the generated summaries further add to it. This confirms that the dual-agent workflow is essential: the Purifier processes raw documents in a separate context , ensuring they never pollute the Reasoner’s attention. 

J Reward Weight Sensitivity Analysis 

To verify that our results are not brittle with respect to the reward scaling factors, we trained a variant with α = β = 0 .15 on the 7B model (a single RL training run requires ∼68 hours on 8 ×A800). The AVG varies by only 0.35%, confirming that the results are not brittle. We attribute this stabil-ity to two factors: (1) the SFT cold-start already provides a strong initialization, so the RL stage primarily reinforces and refines existing behaviors rather than learning from scratch; (2) the auxiliary rewards Rp and Ra are bounded in [0, 1] and scaled by small coefficients, so the answer reward Rans 

always dominates the gradient signal. The auxiliary terms act as gentle regularizers rather than primary optimization drivers.
